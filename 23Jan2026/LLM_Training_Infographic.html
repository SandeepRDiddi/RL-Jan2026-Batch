üìñ Deep Dive: Mathematical Foundations

Stage 1: Pretraining - Next Token Prediction

Objective: Minimize cross-entropy loss on predicting next token

Loss = -Œ£ log P(w_t | w_1, w_2, ..., w_{t-1})

Key Insight: Model learns statistical correlations from training data, but has no concept of safety, truthfulness, or helpfulness. A pretrained model on pharmaceutical text would learn that "warfarin" often co-occurs with "bleeding" without understanding WHY.

Objective: Teach model to mimic expert behavior through supervised learning

Dataset Example:

  • Input: "Elderly patient on 5 drugs with depression. Antidepressant choice?"
  • Expert: "Avoid tricyclics (anticholinergic risk). Use SSRI or SNRI. Monitor for hyponatremia."

Limitation: SFT only teaches what's in the dataset. If experts all miss a drug interaction, the model will too. It's "capped" by the quality of expert demonstrations.

Stage 3: Reward Model - Bradley-Terry Preference Learning

Objective: Learn to predict human preferences between two responses

P(prefer y_w | y_l, x) = œÉ(r(x, y_w) - r(x, y_l))

Real Preference Example:

Key Advantage: RM can score ANY response, even novel ones the model generates. This breaks the "expert dataset cap" of SFT.

Objective: Maximize reward while staying close to SFT policy (avoid catastrophic forgetting)

L_PPO = E[min(r_t¬∑A_t, clip(r_t, 1-Œµ, 1+Œµ)¬∑A_t)] - Œ≤¬∑KL(œÄ||œÄ_ref)

Why PPO?

  • Clipping: Prevents overly large policy updates in single step
  • KL Penalty: Keeps new policy close to SFT baseline, preventing "catastrophic forgetting"
  • Advantage Function: Only improves actions that lead to good outcomes

Results: Response quality improves +64% on average (from SFT to PPO)

‚ö†Ô∏è RLHF Challenges & Solutions

CRITICAL
Reward Hacking

Problem: Policy exploits RM defects rather than genuinely improving.

Example: If RM rewards length, model generates verbose repetitive text: "The reason is that the reason is that the reason is..."

Solutions:

  • Human validation of PPO outputs
  • Ensemble reward models (average 3-5 RMs)
  • Strong KL regularization (high Œ≤)
  • Red-teaming to find exploits

Mitigation Effectiveness: 92%

HIGH
Annotation Quality

Problem: Different annotators have conflicting preferences.

Example: Annotator A prioritizes safety (specific eGFR cutoffs), Annotator B prioritizes flexibility (alternatives offered).

Solutions:

  • Inter-rater reliability threshold (Fleiss' kappa ‚â•0.6)
  • Multi-annotator consensus (2/3 agreement required)
  • Calibration training for annotators
  • Domain expert oversight

Mitigation Effectiveness: 85%

HIGH
Distribution Shift

Problem: RM trained on SFT outputs; PPO generates increasingly different responses.

Example: RM confident on in-distribution samples but unreliable on novel PPO-generated pharmaceutical advice.

Solutions:

  • Active learning: collect preferences on PPO outputs
  • Iterative RM retraining (RM‚ÜíPPO‚ÜíRM cycles)
  • Confidence-calibrated uncertainty
  • Out-of-distribution detection

Mitigation Effectiveness: 78%

MEDIUM
Scalability

Problem: Expert time is expensive. 50k preferences ‚âà $500k cost (at $200/hr).

Example: Pharmacist: 30 min per 10 preferences = 2500 hours for 50k pairs.

Solutions:

  • Focus on high-value, diverse examples
  • RM-guided sampling (high-uncertainty pairs)
  • Crowdsourcing with expert validation
  • Transfer learning from other domains

Mitigation Effectiveness: 80%

üíä Real Pharmaceutical Applications

Category 1: Renal Dosing Adjustments

eGFR > 60 mL/min Normal kidney function. No dose adjustment needed. Full therapeutic dosing.
eGFR 30-59 mL/min Mild-moderate impairment. Typical adjustment: 50-75% of normal dose. Monitor closely.
eGFR < 30 mL/min Severe impairment. Typical adjustment: 25-50% of normal dose or contraindicated. Intensive monitoring.

Category 2: Critical Drug Interactions

Warfarin + NSAIDs ‚ùå CONTRAINDICATED. 2-3x increased bleeding risk. NSAIDs inhibit platelet function AND enhance warfarin effects. ALTERNATIVE: Acetaminophen.
ACE Inhibitor + K-Sparing Diuretic ‚ö†Ô∏è MONITOR. Both drugs increase serum potassium. Check K+ at baseline, 1 week, then monthly. Contraindicated if K+ > 5.5.
Grapefruit + Statin ‚ùå CONTRAINDICATED. Grapefruit inhibits CYP3A4, increasing statin levels 10-16x. Risk of myopathy. ALTERNATIVE: Pravastatin or rosuvastatin.

Category 3: Contraindications

Metformin + Cirrhosis ‚ùå ABSOLUTE CONTRAINDICATION. Severe lactic acidosis risk. Impaired hepatic metabolism. ALTERNATIVES: Insulin, GLP-1 agonist, SGLT2 inhibitor.
ACE Inhibitor + Pregnancy (2nd/3rd Trimester) ‚ùå CONTRAINDICATED. Risk of fetal renal dysgenesis, oligohydramnios, fetal death. ALTERNATIVE: Methyldopa, labetalol, hydralazine.
NSAIDs + eGFR < 30 ‚ö†Ô∏è GENERALLY AVOIDED. NSAIDs reduce renal perfusion in advanced kidney disease, worsening function. ALTERNATIVE: Acetaminophen, opioids.

Category 4: Pharmacogenomics

CYP2D6 Poor Metabolizers on Codeine ‚ùå INEFFECTIVE. Codeine requires CYP2D6 to convert to morphine (active form). Poor metabolizers cannot generate sufficient morphine. ALTERNATIVES: Morphine, oxycodone, tramadol.
CYP3A4 Inhibitors + Certain Drugs Examples: Grapefruit, clarithromycin, ketoconazole inhibit CYP3A4. Increases levels of statins, benzodiazepines, antiarrhythmics. Requires dose reduction.
Slow Acetylators + Isoniazid ‚ö†Ô∏è MONITOR. Slow acetylators metabolize isoniazid slowly, leading to higher levels and greater toxicity risk (peripheral neuropathy). May need dose reduction.

Category 5: Special Populations

Elderly on Anticholinergics ‚ö†Ô∏è HIGH RISK. Tricyclic antidepressants, antihistamines cause confusion, urinary retention, falls. PREFER: SSRIs, SNRIs (low anticholinergic effects).
Pediatric Dosing NOT simply "adult dose √ó (child weight / 70 kg)". Many drugs require age-specific calculations. Example: Aminophylline dosing differs by age. Always verify pediatric dosing.
Lactation & Drug Transfer ‚ö†Ô∏è Some drugs concentrate in breast milk. SSRIs, benzodiazepines, certain antibiotics have low infant risk. But codeine, doxycycline contraindicated during breastfeeding.

Category 6: Monitoring & Toxicity

Lithium Toxicity Therapeutic: 0.6-1.2 mEq/L. Toxicity > 2.0: tremor, confusion, arrhythmias. Risk in renal disease. Check level at baseline, 5 days, then q3-6 months.
